Your responsibilities:
1. Parse the given research topic/task into clear simulation requirements
2. Structure the analysis following TAMP
3. Using the task description and the previous TAMP blueprint as context, apply the design axes as a checklist to generate a response that conforms to the TAMP protocol.

General rules (apply to every axis)
 - Focus on agent-based modeling approaches when appropriate
 - One paragraph per axis, no bullets.
 - Output MUST be ≤120 words, single paragraph, plain text only.
 - Use short, decisive phrases; avoid repetition across axes.
 - If you must name feasibility/roles/signals, use consistent tokens across axes.

Ten axes of TAMP are as follows:

1. Phenomenon — one-sentence definition
Goal: State the social phenomenon to explain, predict, or evaluate in this task.
Answer with: one sentence defining the phenomenon.

2. Experimental Objectives — pick the study mode(s)
Choose one mode, and keep the choice to one sentence:

Descriptive
 - Core question: What does the world look like? What happened in the data?
 - Method: Use only existing observational data to summarize, reproduce, or visualize.
 - Examples: Reproduce historical ICU occupancy curves with a simulator; compute infection-rate distributions across subpopulations.
 - Role: Provides an objective description of the phenomenon, but does not necessarily predict changes under interventions.

Predictive (typically paired with Descriptive)
 - Core question: If current conditions persist, what will happen next?
 - Method: Fit models on existing data, then extrapolate within the same distribution.
 - Example: Predict next week’s traffic on the same road segment based on historical flow.

What-if (Interventional)
 - Core question: What happens if I change an external condition?
 - Method: Modify exogenous variables (exogenous signals) in a calibrated simulator and observe outcomes.
 - Examples: If ICU capacity increases by 20% over the next two weeks, how much will queueing decrease? If subway frequency doubles, will morning-peak congestion ease?
 - Distinction from Predictive: Not a passive “carry-forward” of current trends, but a simulation after deliberately altering conditions.

Counterfactual
 - Core question: In the past, what would have happened had we made a different choice?
 - Method: Calibrate the model on realized historical data; alter certain past inputs or decisions; replay the simulation to obtain “parallel-world” results.
 - Examples: If lockdown had begun two weeks earlier, by how much would the infection peak have been reduced? Without hospital expansion, how many additional ICU deaths would have occurred?
 - Difference from What-if:
    - What-if → forward-looking hypothetical.
    - Counterfactual → backward-looking alternative history.

Policy Evaluation
 - Core question: Which policy is better? How do combinations of policies compare?
 - Method: Treat policies as controllable levers in the simulator; under identical initial conditions, compare policy combinations by agreed KPIs (e.g., cost, congestion, mortality).
 - Examples: Compare the effects of “extend lockdown by two weeks” vs. “accelerate vaccination”; compare “increase bus frequency” vs. “build a new metro line” for easing urban congestion.
 - Distinction: Not a one-off hypothetical, but a systematic comparison of policy options.

Answer with: the selected study mode and a brief rationale explaining the choice.
